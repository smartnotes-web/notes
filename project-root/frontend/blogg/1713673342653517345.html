Heat and Temperature

Heat and Thermometry

↪ The terms temperature and heat are often used with the same meaning in everyday life. But in physics, these two terms have very different meanings. Heat is a form of energy which can be converted into any type of energy.

↪ The unit of heat is joule in the SI system.

↪ The CGS unit is erg.

↪ It is also measured in calorie.

↪ Definition of Heat (Heat energy): It is the transfer of energy between two objects or an object and its surroundings due to temperature difference.

↪ Definition of calorie: It is the required heat energy to raise the temperature of 1 g of pure water through 14.5°C to 15.5°C. The bigger unit is 1 kilo calorie = 1000 calorie. As we add more and more heat to a body, its temperature goes on increasing.

↪ Definition of Kilo calorie: The amount of heat required to increase the temperature of one kilogram of pure water from 14.5°C to 15.5°C is called one kilo calorie. One thousandth part of one kilo calorie is called one calorie.

↪ Temperature:The degree or intensity of heat present in a substance or object, especially as expressed according to a comparative scale and shown by a thermometer.

Modes of Transfer of Heat

↪ There are three different modes of transfer of heat, namely

  1. Conduction
    2.
  2. Convection
    3.
  3. Radiation
    4.

Conduction:

↪ The process in which heat is transferred along a body, from a part of higher temperature to that at lower temperature without bodily movement of the particles of the medium is called conduction.

↪ When a metal rod is heated at one end, the opposite end of the rod gets heated after some time. In this case, the molecules of the rod do not move from their positions, but the heat travels from one end of the rod to the other.

↪ The process of conduction is explained on the basis of the dynamical theory of heat as follows.

↪The molecules of every solid are continuously vibrating about their mean positions. The vibrations of molecules increase with the supply of energy.

↪The molecules of the metal rod near the source of heat are the first to receive heat. As soon as they receive heat, they begin to vibrate vigorously. These molecules collide with the molecules in the neighboring layer and part with some of the heat energy possessed by them. Having received additional energy, the molecules in this layer also begin to vibrate vigorously and in turn give some of the energy with them to the next ones. In this way, heat travels along the rod.

↪The molecules of the solids are held rigidly in their mean position, hence conduction is the only mode of transfer to the heat in solid.

Convection:

↪ In thermal convection, the constituent particles actually move from one place to another. This fact indicates that convection is observed only in gases and liquids i.e., fluids. It is also true that in fluid thermal conduction plays a very small part in heat transfer.

↪ In convection, fluid at the bottom becomes hot and so its volume increases, due to which its density decreases. So due to buoyant force, this 'lighter' fluid moves upwards and due to gravitational effect the fluid with greater density is transferred from the top to the bottom. Due to such a continuous process, the fluid is heated. If you add potassium permanganate into water while heating it in a flask, you can observe this phenomenon.

↪ Convection can be natural as well as forced. If the motion of the material is only due to the difference in density it is natural convection e.g., 'cool current' near a sea shore. The soil becomes hot due to sun rays and so the air in contact with this soil also gets heated and its volume increases and its density decreases. So this air is moving up. So the air pressure near the soil reduces due to which the cool air from sea side rushes towards the soil. Thus cool currents of air are formed. During the night, it reverses.

↪In forced convection, a fluid is forced to move using some appliance, like a pump, or a stirrer. In the human body, the heart acts as a pump for blood circulation. Due to this blood circulation, body temperature is maintained.

Anomalous Expansion of Water:

↪ The volume of water increases instead of decreasing when the temperature of water is reduced from 4°C to 0°C. This is called anomalous expansion of water. The existence of fish and other aquatic animals is possible because of natural convection and anomalous expansion of water. In winter, temperature decreases, the upper layer of water converts into ice and stops the heat from below layers to the atmosphere. So below the ice layer, there is a temperature at 4°C and fish and other aquatic animals can survive.

Radiation:

↪ Every substance emits electromagnetic radiation of certain frequencies in accordance with its temperature. These radiations are thermal radiations. Thermal radiations are electromagnetic radiations only. The energy associated with these electromagnetic radiation is called radiant energy.

Prevost Theory of Heat Exchange

↪ According to Prevost, each substance continuously emits thermal radiations at any temperature. With an increase in temperature, the rate of emission of thermal radiation also increases. Also, the same substance absorbs other radiation incident on it. If the rate of the temperature of the substance increases and if the rate of absorption of the thermal radiation is less than the rate of emission of thermal radiation, the temperature of the substance increases. When the temperature of a substance is the same as that of the environment in which it is kept, the rate of absorption and emission of thermal radiation are equal. The proportion of various frequencies of electromagnetic radiation in thermal radiation depends on the type of material and the temperature of the material.

Perfect Black Body and Black Body Radiation

↪ body which absorbs all the radiant energy incident on it is called a perfect black body. It is impossible to get a 100% perfect black body in nature on the earth.

↪ To study black body radiation, consider a spherical cavity as shown in the diagram. Its inner surface is blackened and rough. It has a small hole. Radiation entering this cavity through the entrance hole undergoes many reflections and each time it is partly absorbed and partly reflected. When it reaches the hole again, it is almost left with no energy. In this sense, this pinhole can be considered a perfect black body. If such a cavity is uniformly heated, radiations coming out of it can be considered to be black body radiation. These radiations are also called cavity radiations.

↪ The sun can be considered as a perfect black body. Black body radiation depends only on the temperature.

Temperature Measurement Scales

↪ A device used to measure a unit real number (i.e., temperature) related to a given thermal equilibrium state is called a thermometer. Thermometer is used to measure hotness or coldness. The commonly used property is the variation of the volume of a liquid with temperature. Mercury and alcohol are the liquids used in most liquid-in-glass thermometers.

↪ Thermometers are calibrated so that a numerical value may be assigned to a given temperature. For calibration of the standard scale, two fixed reference temperatures are required, the freezing point of water (32°F or 0°C) and the boiling point of water (212°F or 100°C) at 1 atmosphere pressure are two convenient points.

↪ The liquid in glass thermometers show different readings for temperatures other than the fixed points because of differing expansion properties of liquids. But the thermometers using gas, at low enough pressure of constant volume for the measurement of temperature, give the same reading regardless of which gas is used.

Celsius Scale:

↪ In this scale, the melting point of ice is taken as 0°C and the boiling point of water is taken as 100°C.

↪ Relation between Kelvin scale and Celsius scale:

    TC = T - 273.15

↪ Where:

  • TC = Temperature on Celsius scale
  • T = Temperature on Kelvin scale

↪ Temperature of triple point of water on Celsius scale (melting point of ice):

    TC = 273.16 - 273.15 = 0.01°C (i.e. approximately 0°C)

↪ Boiling point of water in Kelvin scale:

    T = 100 + 273.15 = 373.15K

Fahrenheit Scale:

↪ In this scale, the freezing point of water (i.e., melting point of ice) is taken as 32°F and the boiling point of water is taken as 212°F.

↪ Relation between Fahrenheit scale and Celsius scale:

    TF = (9/5) × TC + 32

↪ Where:

  • TF = Temperature on Fahrenheit scale
  • TC = Temperature on Celsius scale

Kelvin Temperature Scale:

↪ A given quantity of gas having low enough pressure satisfies the ideal gas state equation:

    PV = nRT

↪ where:

  • n = number of moles of gas
  • R = universal gas constant = 8.31 J mol-1 K-1

↪ Thus keeping the volume of the gas to be constant, it gives P ∝ T. Thus, with a constant volume of gas thermometer, the temperature is read in terms of its pressure. A graph of P - T gives a straight line.

↪ It looks like that the pressure approaches zero with decreasing temperature if the gas continues to be a gas. The absolute minimum temperature for an ideal gas inferred by exploring the straight line to the temperature axis approaches -273.15°C and if it designated as Absolute zero.

↪ Absolute zero is the foundation of the Kelvin scale and is taken as 0 K. The melting point of ice, i.e., 0°C, is taken as 273.15K and the boiling point of water, i.e., 100°C, is taken as 373.15K.

Heat Capacity and Specific Heat:

↪ The ratio of heat Q supplied to a body to a change in its temperature ΔT is called heat capacity HC of the body.

    HC = Q/ΔT

↪ The SI unit is Jk-1 or Cal/K.

↪ Heat capacity of a body depends on the material of the body as well as its mass. Different bodies of the same material but different mass have different values of heat capacity.

↪ The gain or loss of heat continues until the required temperature difference is maintained. During this process, the substance may melt or vaporize.

Specific Heat:

↪ Definition: The quantity of heat required per unit mass for unit change in temperature of a body is called the specific heat of the material of the body.

↪ The unit of specific heat is cal g-1 K-1 or J kg-1 K-1.

↪ Thus specific heat = Heat capacity/Mass

    ∴ C = (Q/ΔT)/m = Q/(mΔT)

    ∴ Q = mcΔT

Specific Heat of Gases:

Molar specific heat:

↪ The amount of heat required to change the temperature of 1 mole of a gas by 1 K (1°C) is called molar specific heat of the gas.

Specific heat at constant volume (Cv):

↪ The amount of heat required to change the temperature of 1 mole of gas by 1 K, keeping its volume constant, is called the specific heat Cv of the gas at constant volume.

Specific heat at constant pressure (Cp):

↪ The amount of heat required to change the temperature of 1 mole of gas by 1 K, keeping its pressure constant, is called the specific heat Cp of the gas at constant pressure.

↪ Relation between Cp and Cv :

    Cp - Cv = R

↪ Where R is universal gas constant.

Coefficient of Thermal Conductivity and its Engineering Applications:

↪ The capacity of a substance to conduct heat is called conductivity. Consider a metal rod of cross-sectional area A. Imagine two parallel planes X and Y anywhere in the rod. Let θ1 and θ2 be the temperature of planes X and Y respectively. Let Q be the quantity of heat conducted normally in t seconds across the plane X. If we assume that there is no loss of heat, the same amount will be conducted across the plane Y.

↪The quantity of heat conducted depends upon the following factors:

  1. The quantity of heat conducted is directly proportional to the cross-sectional area of the rod:

    2. Q ∝ A

  2. The quantity of heat conducted is directly proportional to the temperature difference between the planes:

    3. Q ∝ (θ1 - θ2)

  3. The quantity of heat conducted is directly proportional to the time of flow:

    4. Q ∝ t

  4. The quantity of heat conducted is inversely proportional to the distance between the planes:

    5. Q ∝ 1/d

    Combining we get:

    Q ∝ A (θ1 - θ2) t / d

    i.e. Q = K ∙ A (θ1 - θ2) ∙ t / d            ....... (i)

↪ K is constant, known as coefficient of thermal conductivity, its value depends upon the material of the rod.

↪ In equation (i) if we take:

     A = 1

     (θ1 - θ2) = 1

     t = 1

     d = 1

    ↪ We get Q = ( K × 1 × 1 × 1 )/1

     ∴ Q = K

Coefficient of Thermal Conductivity and its SI Unit:

↪Definition: The thermal conductivity is defined as the quantity of heat flowing normally across a unit cross-sectional area of a material of unit thickness in one second, when the temperature difference between its ends is unity.

↪Unit of K:

  • Cal/cm°Cs - C.G.S.
  • K - Cal/m°Cs - M.K.S.
  • Watt/mK - SI
    •

Temperature Gradient (G):

↪ The quantity (θ1 - θ2)/d, the temperature difference per unit length of the rod, is called temperature gradient. In steady state, the temperature gradient is the same throughout the length of the rod. Let G denote temperature gradient.

↪ From equation (i) we get:

     Q = K ∙ A ∙ G ∙ t

     When A = 1, G = 1, t = 1, ∴ Q = K

↪ The coefficient of thermal conductivity may also be defined as the quantity of heat conducted normally across a unit cross-sectional area in one second, when the temperature gradient is unity.

Applications of thermal conductivity:

  1. Ceiling of house is made up of concrete and conducts more heat. To reduce conduction of heat, a wall of brick is prepared on the outside of the ceiling and a false ceiling of plaster of Paris (POP) is installed inside the ceiling. Thus, the house is kept cool by reducing heat conduction from outside.
  2. Metal handles in doors feel cool in winter, and tiles as well as steel vessels are also felt cool in winter.
  3. Wood and air are bad conductors of heat. If ice is kept in wood, it does not melt for a longer time.
  4. A new blanket gives more heat to the human body as air is trapped in the blanket compared to an old blanket. Two thin blankets give more heat than one thick blanket.
  5. Handles of tea-pots and cookers are made of rubber or wood as they are bad conductors, while the remaining parts are made of metal as metal is a good conductor of heat.

Expansion of Solids, Coefficient of Linear Expansion:

↪ We know that most objects expand when they are heated and contract when cooling. When an object is linear, i.e., it has more length in comparison to thickness, considerable expansion takes place in length when heated. This is called linear expansion.

Coefficient of linear expansion:

↪ If an object is rod-shaped and the temperature difference is small, the change in rate of length (∆l/l) is proportional to the change in temperature T.

     ∴ ∆l/l ∝ ∆T

     ∴ ∆l/l α1 ∙ ∆T

↪ Here α1 is known as coefficient of linear expansion. It is a property of matter. Generally, expansion is more in metals and their value of α1 is high.
