Theory of Communication

↪ Communication is arguably one of the most important aspects of life, whether it is the dark ages or modern-day life.

↪ It is a way to share experiences, ideas, thoughts, and information.

↪ It is what connects us all together.

↪ When we utter the word "communication", we are traditionally thinking about speaking and listening but in reality, communication consists of several activities such as reading, writing, understanding, responding as well as speaking and listening.

Definitions of Communication

  1. Communication is the activity of conveying information through the exchange of thoughts, messages, or information, as by speech, visuals, signals, writing or behaviour. It is the meaningful exchange of information between two groups or a group of persons.
  2. The act or an instance of communicating; the impairing or exchange of information, ideas, or feelings.
  3. A process by which information is exchanged between individuals through a common system of symbols, signs, or behaviour.
  4. Communication is a process of exchanging ideas, thoughts, information, knowledge, etc. between sender and receiver through accepted codes.
  5. Communication is the activity or process of exchanging ideas and feelings or giving people information.

Process of Communication

  1. Ideation: The first step of communication is for the sender to have an idea. This idea has to be sent to the receiver through a proper channel. This first step is called ideation.
  2. Encoding: The idea has to be converted into a message which can then be transmitted, through proper symbols and signs which must be proper and comprehensible to the receiver. This step is called encoding.
  3. Channel: The message must be transmitted through proper channel media/channels. The sender must be extremely careful while selecting the mode of transmission so that there aren't any changes or miscommunication.
  4. Decoding: In this step, the receiver receives the message sent by the sender and then understands the original idea. To do this, the receiver will decode the message. This process is called decoding. After decoding the message, the idea reaches to the receiver.
  5. Receiving: The last step in the one-way process of communication is receiving the idea which the sender earlier had in his mind.
  6. Feedback: In this step of communication, the receiver now has to become the sender and convey back to the sender that the message has been received. They have to go through all the above steps to send their feedback to the earlier sender who is now the receiver. This is the most important step as it ensures completion of the cycle of communication.

Levels of Communication

  1. Extra staff: This level of communication takes place between humans and other species. Example: your cat coming near you or when you pet them.
  2. Intrapersonal: This level of communication takes place within an individual. Self-motivation is an excellent example of intrapersonal communication. Examples: while preparing for an examination, we reassure ourselves with words like 'you can do it' and 'you are prepared'.
  3. Interpersonal: This level of communication occurs between human beings. In this level of communication, the roles of sender and receiver change constantly. Sharing information, exchanging ideas and negotiating are all examples of interpersonal communication.
  4. Organisational: This level of communication takes place between and within organisations. In this type of communication, networks of communications may vary.
  5. Mass: This is when the sender has to reach out to a large mass of people. Examples of this type of communication are: announcements, information sent out through pamphlets, advertisements, etc. In this level, the sender selects an appropriate medium such as newspapers, books, magazines, etc. to reach to the masses.

Verbal and Non-Verbal Communication

↪ There are two major types of communication: verbal and non-verbal communication.

  • Verbal communication is the type of communication in which words are used either in oral or written form.
  • Non-verbal communication is the type of communication in which words are not used.

Components of Non-Verbal Communication

  1. Kinesis: In kinesis, the movement of our body conveys many different and specific meanings whose interpretation may be culture-bound. Since many of these movements occur unconsciously or subconsciously, there is a significantly larger risk of misinterpretation.
  2. Proxemics: Proxemics means distance. The measure of distance we keep from others conveys the relationship we share with them or the subject we are discussing.
  3. Chronemics: Chronemics deal with time, pauses, and stops which play a significant role while communicating.
  4. Paralanguage: Paralanguage deals with vocal qualities such as volume, tone, rate, pronunciation, pitch and intonation patterns.
  5. Appearance: The appearance of a person during the course of communication reflects upon the person's personality as well as effectiveness of the overall communication.

Barriers to Communication

  • Semantic/Linguistic Barrier: When the speaker uses words or language which the listener does not understand correctly, a linguistic barrier is formed.
  • Physical Barrier: Physical barriers to communication are created by elements such as noise, distance, physical discomfort, etc.
  • Interpersonal Barrier: If the attitude of the speaker and the listener does not synchronize, an interpersonal barrier is created.
  • Psychological Barrier: Elements such as one's emotions and/or attitude can form psychological barriers.
  • Organisational Barriers: Sometimes, in an organization, there might be communication loss due to poor flow of communication, poorly planned infrastructure, or information overload.
  • Mechanical Barrier: Many mechanical factors may be responsible for the break in the communication process.
  • Socio-Cultural Barrier: The source of socio-cultural barriers may be caste divide, class conflict, cross-cultural differences, socio-cultural codes and conducts.
